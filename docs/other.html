12.1 May 2021

  • Bisson JI, Wright LA, Jones KA, et al. Preventing the onset of post traumatic stress disorder. Clin Psychol Rev. 2021;86:102004.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2021.102004
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/2021_05/Bisson. 2021.pdf
    • COMMENT: Review of interventions for preventing PTSD. The authors note there’s not much out there on this topic and that more is needed. The most promising data are for intervening with trauma-focused CBT among those diagnosed with acute stress. Important implications not only for deployment-related experiences but also ACEs and military sexual assault.

  • Vinson SY, Dennis AL. Systemic, racial justice-informed solutions to shift “care” from the criminal legal system to the mental health care system. Psychiatr Serv. 2021:appips202000735.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1176/appi.ps.202000735
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/2021_05/Vinson. 2021.pdf
    • COMMENT: A bit outside of what we do but wanted to share. From the abstract, “Black and Brown people make up more than half of those incarcerated in the United States (despite comprising just 32% of the total population). In the absence of an intersectional, antiracist, structurally informed approach, any attempt by the mental health care system to stem the overrepresentation of people with serious mental illness in the criminal legal system will fail. This article provides an overview of the current mental health care system’s shortcomings in serving this population. It proposes concrete steps to address these shortcomings, with a special focus on race and social determinants of health.”

  • Williams DR, Etkins OS. Racism and mental health. World Psychiatry. 2021;20(2):194-5.

    • COMMENT: Very interesting paper on American attitudes about social determinants of health and health equity. Highly recommend for anyone working on the SDOH projects. From the paper, “our research shows that the dominant narrative about drivers of health and health equity is powerful and resistant to change: Most Americans believe that health is predominantly influenced by individual behaviors and access to health care, as opposed to structural and social factors, and health equity is not a widespread priority… Americans surveyed in 2018 still lack knowledge about the social determinants of health or the role of community on health outcomes”

  • Kelber MS, Liu X, O’Gallagher K, et al. Women in combat: The effects of combat exposure and gender on the incidence and persistence of posttraumatic stress disorder diagnosis. J Psychiatr Res. 2020;133:16-22.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpsychires.2020.12.010
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/DEC/Kelber. 2020.pdf
    • COMMENT: PHCOE work. Analyses the impacts of deployment on PTSD and gender-specific differences on development and progression of PTSD. Some noteworthy findings were that PTSD was less likely to develop after combat for women vs. men (suggesting women have different forms of PTSD trauma) and that men were more likely to have persistent PTSD than women. Very interesting and well-done design/study.

  • McCabe SE, Hughes TL, West BT, et al. Sexual orientation, adverse childhood experiences, and comorbid dsm-5 substance use and mental health disorders. J Clin Psychiatry. 2020;81(6).

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.4088/JCP.20m13291
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/DEC/McCabe. 2020.pdf
    • COMMENT: Nationally representative data showing that sexual minorities are exposed to ACEs much more frequently than their heterosexual counterparts and are at increased risk for SUD/BH comorbidities.

  • Nong P, Raj M, Creary M, et al. Patient-reported experiences of discrimination in the us health care system. JAMA Netw Open. 2020;3(12):e2029650.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2020.29650
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/DEC/Nong. 2020.pdf
    • COMMENT: Cross-sectional national survey documenting experiences of discrimination in healthcare settings. About 1 in 5 had experienced discrimination in healthcare settings, most experiencing it repeatedly. The most common forms (in decreasing order) were: race/ethnic, education/income, weight, sex, and age. Females, younger, poorer, and less healthy patients were more likely to experience discrimination in healthcare settings. Would be curious (as with all of this discrimination work) to see how this plays out in the Army.

  • Biglan A, Johansson M, Van Ryzin M, et al. Scaling up and scaling out: Consilience and the evolution of more nurturing societies. Clin Psychol Rev. 2020;81:101893.

    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/Buxbaum. 2020.pdf

  • Giano Z, Wheeler DL, Hubach RD. The frequencies and disparities of adverse childhood experiences in the u.S. BMC Public Health. 2020;20(1):1327.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-020-09411-z

  • Green DC, Holloway IW, Pickering CE, et al. Group perceptions of acceptance of racial/ethnic, sexual and gender minorities in the united states military. Military Behavioral Health. 2020:1-12.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1080/21635781.2020.1819486
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/10.01.2020 - EndNote Test - DO NOT EDIT/Green. 2020.pdf
    • COMMENT: A cool study that looked at perceived acceptance of minority groups. From the paper, “results show members of minority groups tended to perceive acceptance of both their minority group and other minority groups as lower compared with perceptions of acceptance among majority groups.”

  • Liu JJW, Ein N, Gervasio J, et al. Comprehensive meta-analysis of resilience interventions. Clinical Psychology Review. 2020:101919.

    • LINK (doi): http://doi.org/10.1016/j.cpr.2020.101919
    • FILE PATH: P:/CPHE/DEDS/BSHOP/Shared/BH and SH literature/Resources/10.01.2020 - EndNote Test - DO NOT EDIT/Liu. 2020.pdf
    • COMMENT: Resilience is a target for much of the Army’s prevention efforts and we’ve showed its associated with reduced suicidal ideation. This meta-analysis reviewed the evidence for whether interventions can increase resilience. From the paper: “resilience-promoting interventions yielded a small, but statistically significant overall effect, Hedges’s g = 0.48 (SE = 0.04, 95% CI = [0.40, 0.56]. The variability in study effect sizes within and between studies was significant, p < .001, with many falling short of the threshold for practical significance.”
